Personal Experience in STEM

Who am I

I'm Siyuan, pursuing a double degree in Computer Science and Math at the University of Maryland, College Park. Recently, I also accept the Ph.D offer to continue my research in the field of Computer Vision. I want to share my personal experience as a STEM major student in undergraduate CS courses and graduate TA office.

What I see

The Maryland Center for Women in Computing states that “20% of our undergraduate students are women majoring in computer science.” This figure aligns with my impression of the introductory computer science classes. Lower-level CS classes, CMSC131 and CMSC132, should have the highest percentage of female students as all cs students are required to take and they are the mandatory courses for any who want to enter the CS major. When I was taking a high-level 400 course, I counted that 4 out of 30 students were female. This is very alarming figure. UMD claims to have the most number of female students. This means that there are many other university and college that has much worse ratio.

When it comes to graduate school, things get worse. I served as an undergraduate research assistant for the past two years so I constantly visit the graduate student office. Although the following figures may not be representative of the department or all STEM majors, only 2 graduate students are working in that office which contains about 20 seats. This is also an example of the leaky pipeline phenomena. As female students enter the CS major, some of them drop out of the major as courses get harder. Even less female students decide to continue their study in graduate school.
